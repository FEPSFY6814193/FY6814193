History and Evolution

The history of asphalt dates back to ancient civilisations, such as the Egyptians and Babylonians. During the 18th century, the first commercial asphalt road was laid in Paris using natural asphalt. The industry's evolution during the 19th and early 20th century shifted the production of asphalt, which had been mainly derived from natural resources (unique paving materials, 2015). The rise of the automobile industry drove the demand for asphalt, which was already widely used for its smooth and resilient surface. Technological developments over the years have resulted in new additives that can improve its environmental friendliness and durability. As a result, asphalt continues to be a vital component of road construction (unique paving materials, 2015).

Benefits of Using Asphalt for Roads

Asphalt can withstand the effects of weather and traffic because of its adaptable and strong qualities (apai, 2023). Long-lasting, low-cost, and simple-to-maintain asphalt roads can be achieved through repair and maintenance (apai, 2023). Additionally, this material can aid in reducing road noise for instance “the M6 Toll uses a noise-reducing asphalt that significantly reduces the environmental impact of the highway on adjacent” (ppiaf.org, 2009), which is particularly beneficial in creating a calm atmosphere (eapa.org, 2023). The ability of asphalt to set quickly during the construction process helps minimise delays and disruptions in the flow of traffic. It also ensures the smooth and uninterrupted operation of society's activities (eapa.org, 2023).

Concrete Roads: Pros and Cons

The structural strength of concrete roads is what makes them stand out from the rest. Their rigid construction allows them to endure the wear and tear of time. Also, their natural reflective surface helps improve visibility in the dark (perrin, 2023). Concrete roads can still be challenging to maintain due to their various characteristics. One of these is their susceptibility to damage caused by wintry weather. This vulnerability can affect the integrity of the concrete structure (perrin, 2023). Concrete roads are noisier than asphalt roads, a notable case is the M25 motorway in Surrey, where its concrete surface has led to significant noise pollution (Dr Ben Spencer, 2023). The interaction between vehicle tires and the concrete significantly disturbs residents nearby, highlighting the noisier nature of concrete compared to asphalt (Dr Ben Spencer, 2023). Concrete roads offer powerful endurance and strength. But they also face challenges in terms of environmental and maintenance issues (DASWELL, 2022).

Economic Implications

The economics of maintaining and building roads are often complex, as they involve both long-term investments and initial costs. Asphalt can provide immediate benefits, Its maintenance also helps maintain a budget-friendly road system (DASWELL, 2022). Compared to asphalt, concrete is more expensive. This material also requires a larger upfront investment. The maintenance of concrete roads typically leads to grand and costly productions that require a lot of resources and lengthy road closures (perrin, 2023). The convenience and productivity of asphalt contribute to its smooth and economical appearance. Concrete can introduce a bit of dissonance in the economic cost of its higher maintenance costs (Linkedin, 2023).

Environmental Considerations

Asphalt is more eco-friendly than concrete. It can be reused and recycled, which helps minimise waste and conserve resources. Also, it absorbs less heat, which helps maintain a cool urban environment (Linkedin, 2023). Asphalt tends to release more greenhouse gases when it's produced, which can contribute to climate change and affect air quality. Concrete roads have better albedo effects, which help absorb less heat and reflect sunlight (Linkedin, 2023). Concrete production is resource-intensive, and it also has an extensive impact on the environment. Both concrete and asphalt have their distinct environmental challenges that need to be evaluated to make informed decisions (for construction pros, 2023).
